Synopsis

Ninety-Nine Days at Sea

Lujain opens on day ninety-three of fifteen-year-old Lujain Al-Masri's ordeal, alone on the Pacific Ocean. Dehydrated, starving, barely coherent, she has made her decision. The only thing that interrupts her is Najma — a bottlenose dolphin who has sustained her with fish and constant presence for three months.

The novel moves between this survival narrative and the events that brought her there: a Palestinian-American family arrested in Philadelphia, deported under a sweeping executive order, placed aboard a failing coastal vessel. In the night, armed men board. Lujain's mother hides her beneath a bench. A bullet passes through a water jug, its force deflected before striking wood. When she wakes, she is alone.

What follows is both a survival story and a reckoning — with grief, with silence, with what it costs to endure a world that did not come looking for you.

Foreword

In Her Own Words

I remember standing in the middle of Washington, D.C., holding a sign I had made myself, surrounded by thousands of people who were angry and heartbroken and refusing to be quiet.

I was fourteen years old. My throat was raw from chanting — FREE FREE PALESTINE. My feet hurt, and there were so many people I felt like I might be crushed. And somewhere in all of that noise and pain and hope, a story found me.

We had driven from Ohio that morning, my family packed into the car before sunrise. My father was behind the wheel, all of us quiet in the way you are when something feels important.

I am Arab-American. I was not born in America, but I have grown up there. It is the only home I have known in the way a teenager knows home: through school hallways, inside jokes, and the quiet embarrassment of your name being mispronounced so many times you start flinching before attendance even begins.

I grew up watching the news with my father. He would explain things to me — history, politics, the context the television never had time for. He had lived in the Arab world for most of his adult life, and he understood things from the inside that most Americans only see from the outside.

The week before the protest, my family and I watched a documentary about dolphins. I want to be a veterinarian someday. I have always loved animals in the way some people love music — completely, without needing a reason.

There was something about dolphins — their intelligence, the way they communicate, the way they form bonds, the way they sometimes choose to help humans — that stayed with me.

And then Palestine.

To be a teenager in America — fully American — and also know that people who look like your family, who pray like your family, who love their children the way your family loves you, are being told their lives are worthless… it stays with you.

I went to school the next day, and nobody said anything about it. The world just kept moving, and I could not understand how.

"On the drive home from Washington, D.C., somewhere in the dark between the capital and Ohio, with all of that still burning inside me, the story came to me so clearly it felt like something I had always known but never had words for."

I turned to my father and said, "I want to write a story about a Palestinian girl who is lost at sea, and a dolphin saves her. Can you help me?"

He was quiet for a moment, thinking. Then he said, "Tell me more."

So I did. I told him everything. We talked for most of that drive — about a girl who was almost fifteen, like me. About the ocean as a kind of metaphor for everything: the vastness of a world that does not see you, the silence, the endurance it takes to survive.

I had another fear too — quieter, but harder to ignore. I watched videos of ICE taking people, even children, separating them from their families and sending them to countries they had never been to. I was afraid that could happen to us. That we could be taken from our home and thrown away.

This story is fiction.

But the fear is not.
The loss is not.
The silence is not.

I could have been her.

And somewhere, right now, someone is.

If my life had shifted just a few miles — just a few decisions, just a few lines on a map — I could have been the girl the world did not come looking for.

She survived ninety-nine days at sea.

I wrote her so she would not disappear.

Because too many girls like her already have.

Bayan Gray

April 2026  ·  Age 16  ·  Alexandria, Egypt

Origin

How This Novel Was Made

Bayan wrote the first draft of Lujain at fifteen, beginning in the months after that protest in Washington. The story arrived whole — the girl, the ocean, the dolphin, the grief — and she dictated its shape to her father on that long drive home through the dark.

Her father is a writer and English teacher who has spent nearly two decades living and working in the Arab world, first as a teacher, then as a father to six children raised between cultures. He brought to the collaboration not a ghost-writer's hand but a craftsman's one — shaping the manuscript around what his daughter had already seen, already understood, already needed to say.

They worked the way writers work: arguing over scenes, questioning motives, asking what would she actually do? and would she think that yet? and does the dolphin need to be explained, or is the relationship enough? The answer to that last question, they agreed, was always: the relationship is enough.

Bayan researched bottlenose dolphin behavior obsessively. She called the survival elements, cross-checked the geography, and insisted on emotional honesty that did not look away. Her father helped her find the language for what she already knew.

The result is a novel that reads the way grief actually moves — not in a straight line, not toward resolution, but in circles that slowly widen until, finally, there is room to breathe.

About

James Gray, Co-Author

James Gray is an American Muslim revert, teacher, and father of six, currently based in Cairo. He contributed to this novel as collaborator, craftsman, and — most importantly — as Bayan's father. He prefers to let the book speak for itself.
