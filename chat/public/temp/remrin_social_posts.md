## POST 1 — The Launch Post
### Platform: LinkedIn (primary) / Facebook
### Tone: Warm, story-driven, slightly funny at the end

---

There's a Golden Retriever named Max.

Max has a problem. The problem is chocolate. Specifically, that it exists in the world and he can find it anywhere — the trash, the counter, that one low shelf in the pantry his owner swears he can't reach.

His owner, Patty, calls the vet approximately every time Max breathes near anything brown.

Patty is a lot. But Patty is also someone who loves her dog with her whole heart, gets anxious fast, and needs to be reassured *before* she asks the question — because the anxiety is about love, not drama.

The vet knows this. Not from the chart. Not from a database field called "owner: anxious." From three years of consultations. From memory. From the kind of professional knowledge that lives in a person's head and disappears the day they move to another practice.

We built Remrin so that knowledge doesn't disappear.

Remrin is a memory layer for professionals — a system that remembers the human side of every client relationship. The things the chart doesn't hold. The things that make the difference between a consultation that feels clinical and one that feels like you're being cared for by someone who actually *knows* you.

It works alongside whatever AI you already use. It doesn't replace your records system. It adds the one thing records systems have never been able to store:

The person behind the file.

**Remrin — The Human Layer.**
*Remember everything. Miss nothing. Be the professional your clients never forget — because you never forgot them.*

## POST 2 — The Dr. Ahmed Post
### Platform: LinkedIn
### Tone: Professional, emotionally resonant, credible

---

Dr. Ahmed has fourteen patients before noon.

Patient seven is Jane. He last saw Jane three months ago. Before he walks into her room, he opens his laptop and types six words to his AI assistant:

*"We're seeing Jane Doe today. Summary?"*

The response takes two seconds:

*Jane Doe, 43. Construction company manager. Three kids. Husband Robert is also your patient — today is actually his birthday. Last visit: left hand pain, suspected advanced carpal tunnel. She's been avoiding the surgery conversation — mentioned not wanting to be incapacitated while things are stressful at work. She responds well to direct consultations. Doesn't like being lectured.*

Dr. Ahmed didn't file that information. He didn't update a structured database. It accumulated naturally — a comment here, an observation there — over three months of conversations about Jane.

His AI assistant remembered it.

Not because Dr. Ahmed has a better memory system than his colleagues. Because he has Remrin.

Remrin is the infrastructure that closes that gap.

### Platform: Instagram / Facebook
### Tone: Warm, funny, deeply relatable to service professionals

---

Margaret has been coming to the salon for eleven years.

She's 74. She's particular about her colour — not difficult, just precise. She had one bad experience with a new stylist years ago who didn't listen, and she's mentioned it exactly three times since, which for Margaret means it really mattered.

She books at 10am because she likes to be home for lunch. She takes her coffee with oat milk now, not regular, since January. Her granddaughter just started university. Her husband Gerald passed two years ago and she doesn't bring it up unless she's having a hard week — but when she does, she needs someone to just listen, not fix.

You don't learn this from a booking system.

You learn this from eleven years of showing up and paying attention.

And then one day the person who paid that attention leaves the salon, and the next stylist starts from zero, and Margaret — who has been coming here for ELEVEN YEARS — has to explain herself again to someone who doesn't know her yet.

That moment, right there, is what Remrin prevents.

Remrin is a memory layer for professionals. It remembers the human side of every client relationship — the preferences, the history, the things that make someone feel known rather than processed.

So when a new team member sits down with Margaret, they already know.
